Common Fee Models Used by Finance Consultants

Finance consultants typically use one of several fee structures. Understanding these models helps you evaluate what you're paying for and how it impacts your overall investment returns.

Hourly Fees

Some consultants charge by the hour for their time and expertise. This model works well for clients who need specific advice or periodic consultations rather than ongoing management. Hourly rates typically range from $150 to $400 per hour, depending on the consultant's experience and geographic location.

This structure offers flexibility and can be cost-effective for straightforward investment questions or short-term projects.

Flat or Project-Based Fees

For defined projects like creating a comprehensive investment plan or conducting a portfolio review, consultants may charge a flat fee. These fixed-price arrangements typically range from $1,000 to $5,000 or more, depending on the scope and complexity of the work.

Flat fees provide clarity and predictability, allowing clients to know exactly what they'll pay before engaging services.

Assets Under Management (AUM) Fees

The most common model for ongoing investment management is the AUM fee, where consultants charge a percentage of the assets they manage on your behalf. Industry-standard AUM fees typically range from 0.5% to 2% annually, with larger portfolios often qualifying for lower percentage rates.

For example, a 1% AUM fee on a $500,000 portfolio would equal $5,000 per year. This model aligns the consultant's compensation with your portfolio's performance and growth.

Retainer or Ongoing Advisory Fees

Some finance consultants offer retainer arrangements where clients pay a monthly or quarterly fee for continuous access to advice and services. Retainers typically range from $2,000 to $10,000 annually, depending on the level of service and complexity of the client's financial situation.

This model provides ongoing support without tying fees directly to asset values, which can benefit clients with significant non-investment financial needs.

What Influences Consulting Fees?

Several factors determine what a finance consultant charges for investment advice. Experience and credentials play a significant role—seasoned professionals with advanced certifications and proven track records often command higher fees than newer advisors.

The complexity of your financial situation also matters. Clients with multiple investment accounts, business interests, estate planning needs, or specialized tax considerations typically require more sophisticated guidance, which is reflected in pricing.

Geographic location affects rates as well. Consultants in major metropolitan areas generally charge more than those in smaller markets, though virtual advisory services have begun to blur these distinctions.

Transparency and Fiduciary Responsibility

When evaluating finance consultants, ask directly about their fee structure and whether they operate as fiduciaries. Fiduciary advisors are legally obligated to act in your best interest, which includes providing transparent disclosure of all fees and potential conflicts of interest.

Don't hesitate to request a clear, written explanation of what you'll pay and what services you'll receive in return. Reputable professionals welcome these conversations and view transparency as fundamental to building client trust.

Questions to ask include: How are you compensated? Do you receive commissions from recommended products? Are there any additional fees I should know about? Will my fees change as my portfolio grows?
